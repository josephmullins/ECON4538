Connecting Theory with Evidence: The Safety Net

Overview

  • We’ve studied theoretical models of human capital formation and mobility
  • Today: empirical evidence on how policy affects skill formation
  • Focus: social safety net programs
  • Key questions:
    • Do income transfers affect child development?
    • What are the long-run effects on education and earnings?
    • How do these findings connect to our theoretical models?

A Brief Recap

  • We found that for unconstrained households, optimal investments are not affected by economic resources.
    • Finding effects of resources / interventions, a sign of constraints \(\Rightarrow\) welfare and efficiency gains
  • But would it be a good idea to simply do OLS? \[ \log(H_{t+1}) = \beta_{0} + \beta_{1}\log(\Omega_{t}) + \epsilon_{t} \]
  • No!! e.g. \(z_{t-1}\rightarrow \Omega\) and \(z_{t}\rightarrow H_{t+1}\)

The Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)

What is the EITC?

  • Refundable tax credit for low-income working families
  • Phases in with earnings, plateaus, then phases out
  • For families with children, substantial benefits (max ~$7,000)
  • Key features:
    • Conditional on working (labor supply incentive)
    • Varies with number of children
    • Expanded significantly in 1993
  • A few ways to use this variation

EITC Schedule

  • Three regions:
    1. Phase-in: credit increases with earnings
    2. Plateau: credit remains constant
    3. Phase-out: credit decreases with earnings
  • Creates variation in income while holding work incentives roughly constant

Dahl and Lochner (2012)

Research Question

  • Does family income causally affect child achievement?
  • Previous research showed correlations, but hard to separate:
    • Direct income effect
    • Selection (parents with higher income have other advantages)
  • Innovation: use EITC expansions as exogenous income shocks
  • Focus on test scores as outcomes

Identification Strategy

  • Compare children in families eligible for large vs small EITC increases
  • Key variation: 1993 EITC expansion differentially affected families by initial earnings level
  • Use predicted income growth conditional on earnings as an instrument for actual income growth
  • To use this variation, must also control for initial earnings

Main Results

From (Dahl and Lochner 2012):

  • Effect size: $1,000 increase in income → 6% of standard deviation increase in test scores
  • Effects concentrated in math and reading
  • Larger effects for:
    • Younger children
    • More disadvantaged families

Bastian and Michelmore (2018)

Long-Run Effects of EITC

(Bastian and Michelmore 2018) ask: what are the lifetime impacts of EITC exposure?

  • Follow children exposed to EITC expansions into adulthood
  • Outcomes:
    • Educational attainment
    • Employment
    • Earnings
  • Key insight: childhood income may have persistent effects

Identification Strategy

  • Exploit state-level EITC programs (additional to federal)
  • Timing: states adopted at different times
  • Compare children in same state, different birth cohorts
  • State, year, family size \(\rightarrow\) different max credit

Variation in EITC Exposure

Source: (Bastian and Michelmore 2018, fig. 2)

Specification

\[ Y_{i} = \beta_{1}EITC_{i,0-5} + \beta_{2}EITC_{i,6-12} + \beta_{3}EITC_{i,13-18} + \eta X_{i} + \psi V_{s,t} + \lambda Z_{s} + \pi W_{t} + \epsilon_{i} \]

  • \(EITC_{i,-}\): maximum available EITC credit given state, year, family size
  • \(X_{i}\): family characteristics
  • \(Z_{s}\): state fixed effects
  • \(W_{t}\): year fixed effects
  • \(V_{s,t}\): state-year policy variables

Main Findings

  • $1,000 increase in EITC exposure (age 13-18) leads to:
    • 1.3 percentage point increase in high school completion
    • Higher college attendance
    • 2% increase in earnings in mid-20s
  • The EITC is a work incentive and had an effect on maternal labor supply
    • Changes in money and time with children

Barr, Eggleston, and Smith (2022)

Tax Benefits and the Birthday Lottery

(Barr, Eggleston, and Smith 2022) exploit a natural experiment: the January 1 birthdate cutoff

  • U.S. child tax benefits determined by December 31 cutoff
  • Child born December 31 → family gets full tax year benefits
  • Child born January 1 → family waits a year for benefits
  • Natural randomization: due date doesn’t predict birth date precisely
  • Average difference: ~$1,300 for low-income families (~10% of annual income)

The Cash Transfer Discontinuity

Source: (Barr, Eggleston, and Smith 2022, fig. I)

Research Question

Do cash transfers in the first year of life affect long-run child outcomes?

  • Focus: transfer received (or not) right after birth
  • Compare children born just before vs just after January 1
  • Innovation: use universe of administrative tax data
  • Follow children into adulthood (measure earnings in 20s)
  • Also examine intermediate outcomes (test scores, graduation)

Identification Strategy

  • Key assumption: children born Dec 31 vs Jan 1 are similar except for transfer timing
  • Potential threats:
    • C-sections scheduled to manipulate birth date?
    • Response: show no bunching around January 1
    • Different demographics around cutoff?
    • Response: show balance on observables
  • Window: focus on births within days of cutoff

Main Results

Children who received ~$1,300 transfer in first year show:

  • Long-run earnings: 1-2% higher at age 20-25
    • Larger effects for males (3-4%)
    • Concentrated among low-income families
  • Intermediate outcomes:
    • Higher math and reading test scores
    • Increased high school graduation rates
  • Effects emerge early and persist

Return on investment: $1,300 transfer yields increased lifetime earnings far exceeding cost

Mechanisms and Timing

Why do transfers in infancy matter?

  • Critical period: first year of life is developmentally important
    • Brain development
    • Nutrition and health
    • Parent-child bonding
  • Families may be especially credit-constrained after birth
  • Small amounts can have large effects if spent on high-return investments

Food Stamps: Bailey et al. (2024)

The Food Stamp Program

  • In-kind transfer program (now called SNAP)
  • Provides food benefits to low-income households
  • Rolled out county-by-county between 1961-1975
  • Research opportunity: quasi-experimental variation in timing of access

Food Stamp Program Rollout

Source: (Bailey et al. 2024, fig. 1)

Bailey et al. (2024) Study Design

(Bailey et al. 2024) leverage rollout timing:

  • Compare cohorts born just before vs just after Food Stamps arrived
  • Within-county variation in exposure by birth year
  • Link historical rollout data to modern outcomes
  • Long-run outcomes: health, education, economic self-sufficiency in adulthood

Specification

\[ Y_{cbt} = \theta_{c} + \delta_{s(c)b} + \psi_{t} + X_{cb}\beta + \sum_{a=-5}^{a=17}\mathbf{1}\{FS_{c}-b = a\} + \epsilon_{cbt} \]

  • \(c\): county
  • \(b\): birth year
  • \(t\): year of observation
  • \(FS_{c}\): year in which country \(c\) gets FS program
  • \(X_{cb}\) :county-by-birth year covariates

Logic of Study Design

Main Findings

Childhood access to Food Stamps led to:

  • Health: reduced metabolic syndrome, obesity in adulthood
  • Education: increased high school completion
  • Economic: higher earnings, less likely to receive government assistance
  • Intergenerational: children of treated individuals have better outcomes
  • Effects larger for:
    • More disadvantaged families
    • Exposure during early childhood and in utero

Interpretation: nutrition and food security in childhood have lifelong consequences

Event Study: Example

Source: (Bailey et al. 2024, fig. 3)

Return on Investment

  • Authors calculate cost-benefit ratio
  • Benefits from:
    • Higher earnings (increased tax revenue)
    • Better health (lower medical costs)
    • Less transfer receipt (lower government spending)
  • Find ROI of approximately 56 cents per dollar spent
  • Interpretation: Food Stamps are an investment not just consumption

Carneiro et al. (2021): Nigeria Cash Transfer Program

The Child Development Grant Programme (CDGP)

(Carneiro et al. 2021) study a multifaceted intervention in Northern Nigeria:

  • Information provision to mothers and fathers on pregnancy and infant feeding practices
  • Cash transfers to mothers: $22/month (85% of women’s earnings)
  • Transfers begin during pregnancy, continue until child is 24 months
  • Implemented at village level, targeted to pregnant women
  • Randomized controlled trial with 3,688 pregnant women at baseline

Why This Context Matters

  • Northern Nigeria: extreme poverty (70% below $1.90/day)
  • High stunting rates (67% of children under 5)
  • Infant mortality: 90 per 1,000 births
  • Low knowledge of child nutrition practices
  • Women retain control over resources they bring into household
  • Agricultural lean season creates food insecurity

Main Results

  • Child anthropometrics (2 years post-intervention):
    • Height-for-age Z-score increases by 0.22σ
    • Stunting reduced by 8%, extreme stunting by 15%
    • Effects sustained at 4 years
  • Child health:
    • 18% reduction in diarrhea episodes
    • Increased vaccination and deworming rates
  • Mother’s labor supply:
    • 21% increase in earnings at 2 years, sustained at 4 years
    • Increased livestock ownership (key for protein-rich diets)
  • Cognition
    • Effect on communication, but fade-out

Long-Run Impacts and Mechanisms

  • Key mechanisms:
    • Increased parental knowledge and improved practices (breastfeeding, antenatal care)
    • Women invest transfers in livestock assets → protein-rich foods for children
    • Labor supply increases (no reduction despite transfers)
    • Food security improves, especially during lean season
  • Sustained effects: impacts persist 2 years after transfers end
  • Return on investment: Internal rate of return 5-20% depending on assumptions

Interpreting Through Our Models

  • Becker-Tomes: intervention relaxes budget constraints AND provides information
  • Evidence of binding credit constraints for low-income families
  • Technology of skill formation (Cunha-Heckman):
    • Early investments matter (impacts on gestation, early nutrition)
    • Multiple inputs: cash enables protein-rich foods, better practices
    • Dynamic complementarity: early health + continued investment
  • Unique insight: Cash transfers enable productive investments (livestock) that generate sustained income

Synthesis and Implications

What Have We Learned?

  1. Income matters: Causal evidence from EITC, PFD, Food Stamps
  2. Timing is critical: Largest effects for early childhood exposure
  3. Long-run effects: Childhood resources affect adult outcomes
  4. Multiple channels: Income, information, parenting, health all matter
  5. Intergenerational: Effects potentially transmit to next generation

Policy implication: Safety net programs are investments in human capital, not just transfers

Connecting to Theory

These findings validate and extend our models:

  • Credit constraints bind: low-income families would invest more if they could
  • Self-productivity: early investments have compounding returns
  • Dynamic complementarity: nutrition, health, cognitive stimulation interact
  • Multiple skills: cognitive and non-cognitive both affected
  • Intergenerational transmission: improved child outcomes → improved parenting → next generation

Where do the models need refinement?

  • More explicit modeling of multiple input types
  • Better understanding of production function non-linearities
  • Behavioral aspects (information, salience, planning)

Remaining Questions

  • Mechanisms: exactly how does income translate to better outcomes?
    • Direct purchases (food, books, healthcare)
    • Parental time allocation
    • Stress reduction
    • Neighborhood quality
  • Optimal design: conditional vs unconditional? cash vs in-kind?
  • General equilibrium: what if everyone receives transfers?
  • Cost-effectiveness: which interventions have highest returns?
