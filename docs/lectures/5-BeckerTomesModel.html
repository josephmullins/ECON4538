The Becker-Tomes Model

Overview

  • We have learned that income inequality tends to persist across generations
  • Today: theory of intergenerational transmission
  • The Becker-Tomes model (Becker and Tomes 1979, 1986) is a workhorse framework
  • We’ll study some stylized versions
  • Key questions:
    • Why do earnings regress to the mean?
    • How do parents affect children’s outcomes?
    • What determines the speed of mobility?
  • Building a unified framework for inequality and mobility

The Becker-Tomes Framework

Basic Setup

There are 5 basic ingredients in the model:

  1. In each period \(t\), one generation of adults raises the next generation for \(t+1\) (an “OLG” framework)
  2. Care only about consumption, with an altruistic weight \(\beta\) on future generations
  3. Can transfer wealth through:
    1. Financial assets \(A_{t}\), with rate of return \(r\); or
    2. Human capital, \(H_{t}\), with price \(w\) (the wage)
  4. A production function converts investments \(X_{t}\) into human capital \(H_{t+1}\)
  5. An ability endowment, \(z_{t}\), evolves exogenously.

A Simple Version

Here is a simple version that captures important forces:

  • Ability is mean-reverting: \[ z_{t+1} = \rho z_{t} + \eta_{t+1},\ \rho \in(0,1) \]
  • Human capital production: \[ H_{t+1} = \exp(\mu + z_{t})X_{t}^{\delta} \] where \(X_{t}\) has price \(\psi\)
  • Budget constraint: \[ C_{t} + \frac{A_{t+1}}{1+r} + \psi X_{t} \leq A_{t} + wH_{t} \]

A recursive formulation

Let \(\Omega_{t} = A_{t} + wH_{t}\) be total wealth in period \(t\):

\[ V(\Omega_{t},z_{t}) = \max_{C_{t},X_{t},A_{t+1}}\left\{ u(C_{t}) + \beta \mathbb{E}[V(\Omega_{t+1},z_{t+1})] \right\} \]

subject to:

\[ \begin{eqnarray} C_{t} + \psi X_{t} + \frac{A_{t+1}}{1+r} \leq \Omega_{t} \\ \Omega_{t+1} = A_{t+1} + wH_{t+1} \\ z_{t+1} = \rho z_{t} + \eta_{t+1} \\ H_{t+1} = \exp(\mu+z_{t})X_{t}^{\delta} \end{eqnarray} \]

. . .

Note: no constraints on borrowing (for now)

Model Solution

  • This is quite a simple model, but we can learn some interesting and useful things from it
  • We’ll show that investment does not depend on wealth or income
  • We’ll derive the IGE in terms of model primitives
  • We’ll look at the effect of innovations in \(z_{t}\) in the long-run

Result 1: Investment does not depend on Income

Steps:

  1. Take FOC wrt \(X_{t}\) and \(A_{t+1}\)
  2. Show that: \[ X_{t} = \left(\frac{\delta w \exp(\mu+z_{t})}{(1+r)\psi}\right)^{1/(1-\delta)} \]
  3. Analyze. Notice no direct effect of income.

Result 2: The IGE

Steps:

  1. Show that \[\log(H_{t}) = c + \frac{1}{1-\delta}z_{t-1}\] where \(c\) is a constant.
  2. Show that \[ \log(H_{t+1}) = \rho\log(H_{t}) + \frac{1}{1-\delta}\eta_{t} + b \] where \(b\) is a constant.
  3. Show that the IGE is equal to \(\rho\).

Result 3: The Long-Run Effects of Good Fortune

What is the long-run effect of an increase in \(z\)?

  • To do this, let’s assume that \(\beta(1+r) = 1\) for simplicity
  • We’ll take a derivative around the deterministic steady state
  • Assume \(u(\cdot) = \log(\cdot)\).

Result 3: The Long-Run Effects of Good Fortune

  1. Show that in general: \[ u'(C_{t}) = \beta(1+r)\mathbb{E}u'(C_{t+1}) \]
  2. Under our assumptions at steady state: \[C_{t}=C_{t+1}=\bar{C}\]
  3. Define \(Z_{t}=\exp(\mu+z_{t})\) and iterate the budget constraint: \[ \sum_{t=0}^\infty \frac{1}{(1+r)^{t}} \bar{C} = \Omega_{0} + \sum_{t=0}\frac{1}{(1+r)^t}\max_{X_{t}}\left[wZ_{t}X^{\delta}_{t}/(1+r) - \psi X_{t}\right] \]
  4. Show that \[ \max_{X_{t}}\left[wZ_{t}X^{\delta}_{t}/(1+r) - \psi X_{t}\right] = \left(\frac{Z_{t}w}{(1+r)}\right)^{1/(1-\delta)}\psi^{-\delta/(1-\delta)}\left(\delta^{\delta/(1-\delta)} - \delta^{1/(1-\delta)}\right)\]

Result 3: The Long-Run Effects of Good Fortune

  1. Hence: \[ \sum_{t=0}^{\infty}\frac{1}{(1+r)^{t}}\bar{C} = \Omega_{0} + B\sum_{t=0}^{\infty}\exp(z_{t})^{1/(1-\delta)} \left(= \Omega_{0}+B \text{ at long-run steady state}\right)\] where \(B\) collects constants in previous expression.
  2. Consider a one-time, small increase in \(z_{0}\), s.t. \(z_{t} = \rho^{t}z_{0}\): \[ \frac{d\bar{C}}{dz_{0}}\Big\vert_{z_{0}=0} = \frac{r}{1+r} \frac{1}{1-\delta} B\sum_{t=0}^{\infty}\frac{\rho^{t}}{(1+r)^{t}} = \frac{1}{1-\delta}\times B\times \frac{r}{1+r-\rho}\]
  3. So \[ fix\frac{d\log(\bar{C})}{dz_{0}}\Big\vert_{z_{0}=0} = \text{Future earnings share of wealth} \times\frac{1}{1-\delta}\times \frac{1+r}{1+r-\rho} \]
  4. Interpret!

Connecting to IGE Estimation

Recall from last lecture we estimated:

\[y_{\text{child}} = \alpha + \beta \cdot y_{\text{parent}} + \varepsilon\]

  • In this model: \(\beta = \rho\) (ability persistence)
  • Independent of technology parameter \(\delta\)
  • Independent of prices \(w\), \(\psi\), \(r\)
  • Policy invariant: transfers don’t change IGE

. . .

Think: Is this consistent with geographic variation in mobility we saw?

Takeaways so far

  • Investment does not depend on income (when borrowing is unconstrained)
  • Human capital differences fade away, but consumption effects last forever
  • Rich households use wealth bequests to maintain dynasties as human capital fades away
  • IGE determined by ability persistence (\(\rho\)) and human capital production (\(\delta\))
  • But is this realistic? What about credit constraints?

A Two-Period Model

Why Start with Two Periods?

  • Simplify exposition of some key points
  • Two-period version captures key forces
  • Lessons generalize

Two-Period Setup

Parent’s problem in period 1:

\[\max_{C_1, C_2, X} u(C_1) + \beta u(C_2)\]

subject to:

\[ \begin{eqnarray} C_1 + \psi X + \frac{A'}{1+r} &\leq& \Omega \\ C_2 &=& A' + w z X^{\delta} \end{eqnarray} \]

  • \(\Omega = wH + A\): total lifetime wealth
  • \(A'\): bequests (can be negative = borrowing)

Combining Budget Constraints

Combining the two constraints:

\[C_1 + \frac{1}{1+r} C_2 \leq \Omega + \frac{w z X^{\delta}}{1+r} - \psi X\]

  • Left side: present value of consumption
  • Right side: wealth plus net human capital investment
  • Net investment: returns \(\frac{w z X^{\delta}}{1+r}\) but costs \(\psi X\)

First-Order Conditions

Euler equation:

\[u'(C_1) = \beta(1+r) u'(C_2)\]

. . .

FOC with respect to \(X\):

\[\psi = \frac{\delta w z X^{\delta-1}}{1+r}\]

. . .

Same result as before on optimal \(X\).

Investment Does Not Depend on Income (again)

Just as before, we get: \[X^* = \left( \frac{\delta w z}{\psi(1+r)} \right)^{\frac{1}{1-\delta}}\]

With Log Utility

If \(u(C) = \log(C)\), we can solve explicitly:

From Euler equation: \[\frac{C_2}{C_1} = \beta(1+r)\]

Therefore: \[(1+\beta)C_1 = \Omega + \frac{w z X^{*\delta}}{1+r} - \psi X^{*}\]

Where \[X^* = \left( \frac{\delta w z}{\psi(1+r)} \right)^{\frac{1}{1-\delta}}\]

Interpretation

What does this mean economically?

  • Parents optimize to equate marginal returns across uses
  • Rich and poor parents with same \(z\) invest the same in children
  • Differences in income absorbed by \(C_1\), \(C_2\), and \(A\)
  • Crucial assumption: can borrow against future returns to H.C. investment

. . .

Question: Is this realistic? What if parents can’t borrow?

Efficiency and the Social Planner

Is the Market Outcome Efficient?

Consider a social planner who maximizes weighted welfare:

\[\max_{\{C_{i1}, C_{i2}, X_i\}_{i\in[0,1]}} \int \left(\mu_{i1} u(C_{i1}) + \mu_{i2} u(C_{i2})\right)di\]

subject to resource constraint:

\[\int C_{i1} di + \frac{1}{1+r} \int C_{i2} di \leq \int \Omega_i di + \frac{w}{1+r} \int z_i X_i^{\delta} di - \psi \int X_i di\]

. . .

Key question: Does the planner’s solution coincide with the market?

Planner’s First-Order Conditions

The planner’s FOCs are:

\[\frac{u'(C_{i1})}{u'(C_{i2})} = \frac{\mu_{i2}}{\mu_{i1}}, \quad \frac{\delta w z_i X_i^{\delta-1}}{1+r} = \psi\]

Compare to market FOCs:

\[\frac{u'(C_{i1})}{u'(C_{i2})} = \beta(1+r), \quad \frac{\delta w z_{i} X_{i}^{\delta-1}}{1+r} = \psi\]

  • Investment is identical: \(X_i^* = X_i^{planner}\)
  • Only difference: consumption allocation via weights \(\mu_{i1}, \mu_{i2}\)
  • Market outcome is efficient for some choice of weights

Efficiency Interpretation

What does this mean?

  • The market outcome is Pareto efficient
  • “can’t make anyone better off without making someone worse off”
  • Relative to Laissez-Faire economy, Planner can redistribute but that’s it

. . .

But: This relies critically on perfect credit markets!

Incomplete Markets

Can Parents Borrow?

  • Is it really plausible that parents can borrow against returns on their investment in children?
    • And make the children pay for it?
  • No!!
  • All kinds of moral hazard problems + legal issues
  • Also implied no role for income in shaping human capital

A more realistic setup

\[\max_{C_1, C_2, X} u(C_1) + \beta u(C_2)\]

subject to:

\[ \begin{eqnarray} C_1 + \psi X + \frac{A'}{1+r} &\leq& \Omega \\ C_2 &=& A' + w z X^{\delta} \\ A' &\geq& 0 \end{eqnarray} \]

. . .

Positive bequests only!

Inefficient investment

  • This now implies: \[ \frac{\delta w z X^{\delta - 1}}{1+r} \geq \psi \] with equality iff \(A'\geq 0\) and strict inequality if \(A=0\).
  • Equilibriums is inefficient.
  • Introduces a role for policy to improve market outcomes
  • Make everyone better off (Pareto improvement)

When do constraints bind?

  • Solve for \(X^*\) when \(A=0\): \[ \max_{X} u(\Omega - \psi X) + \beta u(wzX^{\delta}) \]
  • With log utility: \[ X^* = \frac{1}{\psi}\frac{\beta\delta}{1+\beta\delta} \Omega \]
  • Note that \(X^{*}\) now depends on \(\Omega\)

When Do Constraints Bind?

The borrowing constraint binds if:

\[\frac{1}{\psi}\frac{\beta \delta }{1 + \beta \delta} \Omega \leq \left( \frac{\delta w z}{\psi(1+r)} \right)^{\frac{1}{1-\delta}}\]

  • More likely to bind when \(\Omega\) is low (poor families)
  • More likely to bind when \(z\) is high (high returns to investment)
  • Creates a region where investment increases with income

Note: High-\(z\) children benefit more from relaxing constraints (steeper slope when binding)

Policy Implications

  • Note that we would like to target transfers based on \(z\)
  • But \(z\) is unobservable
  • An alternative is income-targeted transfers
    • e.g. progressive taxation
  • Can also try direct public investment (e.g. education, early childhood programs)
  • The gains to society depend on the joint distribution of \((\Omega,z)\)
  • What does the evidence say?

What Does the Evidence Say?

In coming lectures we’ll see:

  • Evidence suggests borrowing constraints do bind for many families
  • Returns to college are high, but many don’t attend
  • Educational interventions (especially early childhood) show high returns
  • Suggests scope for welfare-improving policy
