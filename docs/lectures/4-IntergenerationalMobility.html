Intergenerational Mobility

Overview

  • Previous lectures: inequality, welfare, and income processes within individuals over time
  • Today: how does economic status persist across generations?
  • Key questions:
    • How strong is the link between parent and child earnings?
    • Does the U.S. have more or less mobility than other countries?
    • What drives geographic variation in mobility?
  • As we’ll see, mobility connects income processes to long-run inequality.

Motivation

The American Dream

A central tenet of American identity:

“Each generation should do better than the last, regardless of where they start”

  • Does this hold empirically?
  • How much does your parent’s income determine yours?
  • Is the U.S. “the land of opportunity”?

Why Study Intergenerational Mobility?

  1. Normative: Many view high mobility as desirable
    • Equal opportunity vs equal outcomes
  2. Positive: Understanding persistence of inequality
    • Will income disparities persist or fade?
  3. Policy: Identifying effective interventions
    • Education? Early childhood? Neighborhoods?
  4. Theory: Testing models of human capital accumulation

Connection to Income Processes

Recall from Lecture 3:

\[y_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta_i \cdot t + p_{it} + e_{it}\]

  • \(\alpha_i\): permanent individual component
  • Where does \(\alpha_i\) come from?
  • One answer: parental resources, investments
  • Mobility studies: correlation between parent and child \(\alpha\)’s

Measuring Intergenerational Mobility

The Intergenerational Elasticity (IGE)

Most common measure: regression of child log income on parent log income

\[y_{\text{child}, i} = \alpha + \beta \cdot y_{\text{parent}, i} + \varepsilon_i\]

  • \(\beta\) = intergenerational elasticity (IGE)
  • \(\beta = 0\): no relationship (perfect mobility)
  • \(\beta = 1\): perfect persistence (no mobility)
  • Typical estimates: \(\beta \approx 0.4\) to 0.6

Alternative Measure: Rank-Rank Slope

Chetty et al. (2014) popularized rank-based measures:

\[\text{rank}_{\text{child}, i} = \alpha + \beta_r \cdot \text{rank}_{\text{parent}, i} + \varepsilon_i\]

  • Ranks: 0-100 percentile in income distribution
  • \(\beta_r\): how much does parent rank predict child rank?

Empirical Evidence: U.S. Data

Rank-Rank Relationship in U.S. Tax Records (Chetty et al. 2014)

National average slope: \(\beta_r = 0.341\) — remarkably linear across the distribution

Rank-Rank: Interpretation

If \(\beta_r = 0.3\):

  • Child of 25th percentile parent � expects to reach 33rd percentile
  • Child of 75th percentile parent � expects to reach 58th percentile
  • Movement toward middle: mean reversion
  • But substantial persistence remains

Absolute vs Relative Mobility

Two distinct concepts:

  1. Relative mobility: your rank compared to parents (what we’ve discussed)
  2. Absolute mobility: do you earn more than your parents did?

Key insight: Can have high absolute mobility (everyone richer) with low relative mobility (ranks unchanged)

Early Evidence on U.S. Mobility

The Solon-Zimmerman Estimates

Early 1990s: (Solon1992?) and (Zimmerman1992?) used PSID

Key finding: \(\beta \approx 0.4\)

  • Much higher than previous estimates (0.2)
  • Previous work suffered from:
    • Measurement error
    • Life-cycle bias (measuring parents/children too young)

Life-Cycle Bias

Problem: Income varies systematically over life cycle

  • Measuring parent income at age 30 vs 45 gives different results
  • Need to measure at peak earning years (40-50)

Measurement Error Attenuation

Classical measurement error biases IGE toward zero:

\[y_{\text{parent}}^{\text{observed}} = y_{\text{parent}}^{\text{true}} + \nu\]

True model: \(y_{\text{child}} = \alpha + \beta \cdot y_{\text{parent}}^{\text{true}} + \varepsilon\)

But we estimate: \(y_{\text{child}} = \alpha + \beta^* \cdot y_{\text{parent}}^{\text{observed}} + \varepsilon^*\)

  • Result: \(\beta^* < \beta\) (attenuation bias)
  • Solution: Average parent income over many years
  • Or use instrumental variables

Mazumder’s Higher Estimates

Mazumder (2005) used Social Security earnings data:

  • Administrative data: no survey error
  • Long averages: 16 years of parent earnings
  • Result: \(\beta \approx 0.6\) (!!)
  • Substantially less mobility than Solon/Zimmerman

Implication: U.S. mobility lower than previously thought

The Great Gatsby Curve

Corak (2013) documented international relationship:

Key finding: More unequal countries have lower mobility

What Does the Great Gatsby Curve Mean?

Possible interpretations:

  1. Mechanical: Higher inequality � larger gaps to overcome
  2. Investments: Inequality � larger gaps in parental investments
  3. Institutions: Same policies affect both inequality and mobility
  4. Reverse causality: Low mobility � persistent inequality

Geographic Variation: The Equality of Opportunity Project

Chetty et al.’s Data Revolution

Chetty et al. (2014) used de-identified tax records:

  • 40 million children born 1980-1982
  • Linked to parents via tax returns
  • Income measured in 2011-2012 (ages 29-32)
  • Coverage: ~95% of population
  • Geographic detail: Census tract level

Unprecedented scale and precision

National Findings

Using rank-rank specification:

  • National average: \(\beta_r = 0.344\)
  • Interpretation: child at 25th percentile parent rank � expected rank of 33.6
  • Absolute mobility: 50% earn more than parents (declining over time)
  • Remarkably linear relationship across entire distribution

Geographic Variation is Huge

Where is Mobility Highest?

High-mobility areas tend to have:

  1. Less residential segregation (by income and race)
  2. Better K-12 schools (measured by test scores, spending)
  3. Stronger family structure (fewer single parents)
  4. More social capital (civic engagement, church attendance)
  5. Less inequality (smaller Gini coefficient)

These are correlations, not necessarily causal.

Correlates of Upward Mobility

Key Predictors of Intergenerational Mobility (Chetty et al. 2014)

Segregation and Mobility

Correlation: -0.67

School Quality Matters

Test scores predict mobility even controlling for segregation:

  • 1 SD higher test scores � 0.02 higher mobility
  • School spending shows weaker relationship
  • Quality matters more than spending per se
  • But: sorting makes causality difficult

Question: How would you test if this is causal?

Family Structure

Areas with more single mothers have lower mobility:

  • 1 SD more single mothers � 0.03 lower mobility
  • Holds even for children of married parents
  • Suggests neighborhood peer effects
  • Or: family structure proxies for other factors

Moving to Opportunity

Can changing neighborhoods improve outcomes?

Moving to Opportunity (MTO) experiment (Chetty2016?):

  • Randomized housing vouchers to move to lower-poverty areas
  • Children who moved before age 13: large gains
    • 30% increase in earnings
    • Higher college attendance
  • Children who moved after age 13: no gains
  • Suggests critical period for neighborhood effects

The Role of Age

Implication: Early interventions may be more effective

Race and Mobility

Large racial disparities:

  • Black children from high-income families have lower upward mobility
  • Black children from low-income families have similar or lower upward mobility
  • Disparities largest for men
  • Persist even within same neighborhoods
  • Suggests factors beyond neighborhood matter (discrimination, networks)

The Puzzle of Declining Mobility

Is mobility declining over time?

  • Relative mobility (rank-rank): roughly constant since 1980s
  • Absolute mobility: sharply declining
    • 90% of 1940 cohort earned more than parents
    • Only 50% of 1980 cohort earned more than parents
  • Due to: slower GDP growth + rising inequality

Connections to Theory

Linking to Becker-Tomes Model

Simple framework (Becker and Tomes 1979):

\[y_{\text{child}} = h(I_{\text{parent}}, \theta, \varepsilon)\]

  • \(I\): parental investments (money, time)
  • \(\theta\): child ability/endowment
  • \(\varepsilon\): luck/environment
  • Parents choose \(I\) to maximize child outcomes
  • Predicts: IGE depends on returns to investment

What Determines the IGE?

In Becker-Tomes framework:

\[\beta = \frac{\text{var}(I) \cdot \left(\frac{\partial y}{\partial I}\right)^2 + \text{cov}(\theta, y_{\text{parent}})}{\text{var}(y_{\text{parent}})}\]

  • Higher return to investment \(\rightarrow\) higher IGE
  • More inequality in investments \(\rightarrow\) higher IGE
  • More inequality in endowments \(\rightarrow\) potentially lower IGE
  • Connection to income processes: investments affect \(\alpha_i\)

Credit Constraints

If poor families are credit constrained:

  • Cannot invest optimally in children
  • Creates persistence: poor � low investment � poor
  • Rich families invest more efficiently
  • Implication: IGE could be lowered by:
    • Education subsidies
    • Early childhood programs
    • Reducing borrowing constraints

We’ll explore these policies in later lectures.

The Role of Heritability

Genetic vs environmental transmission:

  • Genetic: ability, personality traits
  • Environmental: investments, culture, neighborhoods
  • Evidence: adoption studies show both matter
  • But: gene-environment interactions complicate story
  • Policy can’t change genes, but can change returns to ability

Connecting to Income Processes

Recall from Lecture 3:

\[y_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta_i \cdot t + p_{it} + e_{it}\]

Intergenerational transmission:

  • Child’s \(\alpha_i\) correlated with parent’s \(\alpha_i\)
  • Child’s \(\beta_i\) (growth rate) also correlated with parent income
  • Permanent shocks \(p_{it}\) may be correlated across generations
  • Mobility studies: focus on \(\alpha\) correlation

Policy Implications

What Policies Can Increase Mobility?

Based on evidence:

  1. Early childhood education (critical period effects)
  2. School quality improvements (test scores matter)
  3. Desegregation policies (exposure effects)
  4. Housing vouchers (MTO results)
  5. Income support (reduce credit constraints)

But: need causal evidence on each!

The Equity-Efficiency Tradeoff?

Traditional view: redistribution reduces efficiency

But: if mobility is low due to market failures:

  • Credit constraints � underinvestment in poor children
  • This is inefficient (high-return investments not made)
  • Policies that increase mobility may increase efficiency
  • Depends on whether low mobility reflects optimal responses or constraints

The Role of Place-Based Policies

Evidence suggests where you grow up matters:

  • Move to better neighborhood � better outcomes
  • But: neighborhood characteristics are endogenous
  • Place-based policies:
    • Promise zones, empowerment zones
    • School improvements
    • Infrastructure investments
  • Key question: cheaper to move people or improve places?

Universal vs Targeted Programs

Two approaches:

  1. Universal: benefits for all children (e.g., public schools)
    • Pros: less stigma, political support
    • Cons: expensive, some waste
  2. Targeted: focus on disadvantaged (e.g., Head Start)
    • Pros: cost-effective, high returns
    • Cons: potential stigma, targeting challenges

Measurement and Data Challenges

The Age-30 Problem

Chetty et al. measure children at ages 29-32:

  • Problem: haven’t reached peak earnings yet
  • May understate mobility if poor children have steeper profiles
  • But: measuring later means waiting longer!
  • Mazumder (2018) suggests IGE keeps rising when measured at older ages

Selection and Migration

Who stays in their birth location?

  • Successful children may leave low-mobility areas
  • Makes area look worse than it is
  • Unsuccessful children may return to low-cost areas
  • Chetty et al. can track movers
  • But: more complex to interpret

Recent Extensions

(Chetty2020?) study racial disparities:

  • Black-white gaps persist even for same-income families
  • Gaps larger for men than women
  • Neighborhood-level variation in gaps
  • Intergenerational wealth matters beyond income

Data Limitations

Even with tax data:

  • Cannot observe: wealth, consumption, life satisfaction
  • Top-coding issues at very high incomes
  • Informal sector earnings not captured
  • Intergenerational wealth transmission understudied

Summary

Key Takeaways

  1. U.S. has lower mobility than many developed countries (\(\beta \approx 0.4-0.6\))
  2. Mobility varies dramatically by geography
  3. Strong correlates: segregation, schools, family structure, inequality

Why This Matters

  • Normative: Equal opportunity is stated social goal
  • Positive: Low mobility � persistent inequality
  • Policy: Identifies high-return interventions
  • Connection: Links income processes (Lecture 3) to skills (Lecture 5)
