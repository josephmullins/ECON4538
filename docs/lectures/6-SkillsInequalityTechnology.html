Skills, Inequality, and the Technology of Skill Formation

Overview

  • Last time: The Becker-Tomes model showed how ability transmits across generations
  • Today: What are the skills that drive inequality?
  • Three main points:
    1. Measurable cognitive and noncognitive skills explain life-cycle differences
    2. We can estimate the technology of skill formation (and skills are malleable early in life)
    3. Latent factor models are essential tools for dealing with measurement error
  • Key papers: J. Heckman, Stixrud, and Urzua (2006), Cunha and Heckman (2007), Cunha and Heckman (2008)

Skills and Life-Cycle Inequality

The Importance of Skills

  • Standard human capital theory: education → wages
  • But which skills drive labor market outcomes?
  • Two broad categories emerge:
    • Cognitive skills: IQ, achievement tests, reasoning
    • Noncognitive skills: personality, motivation, self-control
  • Question: Do both types matter for economic outcomes?

What Do We Mean by Noncognitive Skills?

Examples from psychology literature:

  • Locus of control: belief about control over life outcomes
  • Self-esteem: confidence in one’s abilities
  • Time preference: ability to delay gratification
  • Conscientiousness: persistence, organization
  • Emotional stability: managing stress and emotions

These are often harder to measure than cognitive ability, but may be equally important.

Evidence from Heckman, Stixrud, and Urzua (2006)

Key empirical findings using NLSY79 data:

  • Both cognitive and noncognitive factors significantly affect:
    • Wages (obvious)
    • Schooling attainment (medium)
    • Risky behaviors: smoking, crime, teen pregnancy (large!)
  • For many outcomes, noncognitive skills matter more than cognitive
  • The effects persist over the entire life cycle

Measuring Skills: The Challenge

How do we measure cognitive and noncognitive ability?

  • Cognitive: ASVAB battery (Armed Services Vocational Aptitude Battery)
    • Arithmetic reasoning, word knowledge, paragraph comprehension, etc.
  • Noncognitive: Psychological scales
    • Rotter Locus of Control Scale
    • Rosenberg Self-Esteem Scale
  • Problem: Measurement error!
    • Tests are noisy proxies for true underlying ability
    • Cannot simply use test scores as if they were true ability

Wages by Schooling and Cognitive Factor

From J. Heckman, Stixrud, and Urzua (2006):

  • Higher cognitive ability → higher wages at all schooling levels
  • But the gradient is steeper for college graduates
  • This suggests complementarity between skills and education
  • Cognitive skills have bigger payoffs with more schooling

Wages by Schooling and Noncognitive Factor

From J. Heckman, Stixrud, and Urzua (2006):

  • Noncognitive skills also predict wages
  • The pattern is similar: higher noncognitive ability → higher wages
  • Effects are large and persistent
  • For some outcomes (e.g., crime), noncognitive effects dominate cognitive

[Figure would show wage distributions by noncognitive factor quintiles and schooling level]

Educational Attainment and Skills

  • Both cognitive and noncognitive skills predict years of schooling
  • Noncognitive skills are particularly important for college completion
    • Requires persistence, motivation, delayed gratification
  • This helps explain the Becker-Tomes “psychic costs”
    • Not just ability to do the work
    • Also willingness to persist, study habits, etc.

The Technology of Skill Formation

Skills Are Produced

From the Becker-Tomes model, we had: \[ H_{t+1} = f(I_t, \theta_t) \]

  • But what is the technology \(f(\cdot)\)?
  • How do cognitive and noncognitive skills interact?
  • Are there critical periods for investment?
  • Can we compensate for early disadvantage with late investment?

The CES Technology of Skill Formation

From Cunha and Heckman (2007), the technology takes the form:

\[ \theta_{k,t+1} = \left[\gamma_{1k}I_{k,t}^{\phi_k} + \gamma_{2k}\theta_{k,t}^{\phi_k}\right]^{1/\phi_k} \]

where:

  • \(\theta_{k,t}\) is skill \(k\) (cognitive or noncognitive) at age \(t\)
  • \(I_{k,t}\) is investment in skill \(k\) at age \(t\)
  • \(\phi_k\) governs substitutability between early and late investments
  • This is a self-productive technology: skills today produce skills tomorrow

Key Parameters: Elasticity of Substitution

The elasticity of substitution is: \[ \sigma_k = \frac{1}{1-\phi_k} \]

  • If \(\sigma_k\) is high: early and late investments are substitutes
    • Can compensate for poor early environment with later remediation
  • If \(\sigma_k\) is low: early and late investments are complements
    • Early investment is crucial; hard to compensate later
  • Empirical question: What are \(\sigma_{\text{cog}}\) and \(\sigma_{\text{noncog}}\)?

Self-Productivity and Dynamic Complementarity

Two key properties:

  1. Self-productivity: \(\frac{\partial \theta_{k,t+1}}{\partial \theta_{k,t}} > 0\)
    • Skills beget skills
    • Early advantage compounds over time
  2. Dynamic complementarity: \(\frac{\partial^2 \theta_{k,t+1}}{\partial I_{k,t} \partial \theta_{k,t}} > 0\)
    • Early skills make later investments more productive
    • This creates multiplier effects

These properties have profound implications for policy timing.

Critical and Sensitive Periods

From Cunha and Heckman (2007):

  • Critical period: A time when \(\sigma_k \approx 0\) (pure complements)
    • Investment must happen then; cannot compensate later
  • Sensitive period: A time when \(\sigma_k\) is low but not zero
    • Investment is especially productive, but some later compensation possible
  • Evidence: Early childhood is a sensitive period for both skill types
  • But noncognitive skills remain more malleable into adolescence

The Importance of Early Investment

Key finding from Cunha and Heckman (2007):

  • For cognitive skills: \(\sigma_{\text{cog}}\) is low in early childhood
    • Early investments are crucial
    • Difficult to compensate for early deprivation
  • For noncognitive skills: \(\sigma_{\text{noncog}}\) is somewhat higher
    • More room for remediation in adolescence
    • But still important to invest early

Policy implication: Early childhood interventions may have the highest returns.

Cross-Productivity of Skills

Another important feature from Cunha and Heckman (2008):

\[ \frac{\partial \theta_{C,t+1}}{\partial \theta_{N,t}} > 0, \quad \frac{\partial \theta_{N,t+1}}{\partial \theta_{C,t}} > 0 \]

  • Cognitive skills help produce noncognitive skills (and vice versa)
  • This creates synergies across skill dimensions
  • Holistic interventions may be more effective than targeted ones

Latent Factor Models and Measurement Error

The Measurement Problem

Fundamental challenge:

  • We want to study the effect of true skills \(\theta\) on outcomes \(Y\)
  • But we only observe noisy measures \(M\) of skills
  • If we use \(M\) instead of \(\theta\):
    • Attenuation bias: underestimate true effects
    • Omitted variable bias: if measures are imperfect proxies
  • Classical measurement error: \(M = \theta + \varepsilon\)
    • Bias toward zero: \(\text{plim}\,\hat{\beta} = \beta \frac{\text{Var}(\theta)}{\text{Var}(\theta) + \text{Var}(\varepsilon)}\)

The Latent Factor Approach

Key idea from J. Heckman, Stixrud, and Urzua (2006):

  1. Assume there are \(K\) latent factors \(\theta = (\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_K)\)
  2. We observe \(J\) measurements \(M = (M_1, \ldots, M_J)\)
  3. Each measurement loads on the factors: \[ M_j = \mu_j + \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_{jk}\theta_k + \varepsilon_j \]
  4. Use multiple measures to identify the factors
  5. Correct for measurement error in estimating effects on outcomes

Factor Model Structure

The system has three components:

Measurement equations: \[ M_j = \mu_j + \alpha_{j1}\theta_1 + \alpha_{j2}\theta_2 + \varepsilon_j, \quad j = 1, \ldots, J \]

Outcome equations: \[ Y = X\beta + \gamma_1\theta_1 + \gamma_2\theta_2 + u \]

Choice equations: \[ S = \mathbb{1}[Z\delta + \lambda_1\theta_1 + \lambda_2\theta_2 + v > 0] \]

where \(\theta_1, \theta_2\) are latent factors (e.g., cognitive and noncognitive ability).

Identification Strategy

How do we identify this model?

  1. Normalizations: Set scale of factors
    • E.g., \(\alpha_{11} = 1\), \(\alpha_{22} = 1\)
  2. Exclusion restrictions: Some measurements don’t load on all factors
    • Cognitive tests only load on \(\theta_1\)
    • Personality scales only load on \(\theta_2\)
  3. Multiple measurements: Need \(J \geq 2K + 1\) typically
  4. Covariance structure: Factor model implies testable restrictions

What the Factor Model Buys Us

  1. Correct inference about effects of skills on outcomes
    • No attenuation bias from measurement error
  2. Decomposition of outcomes into skill components
    • How much is due to cognitive vs. noncognitive?
  3. Predicted factors for each individual
    • Can use in subsequent analysis
  4. Tests of model assumptions
    • Overidentification restrictions

Example: NLSY79 Data

J. Heckman, Stixrud, and Urzua (2006) use:

Cognitive measurements: - ASVAB: Arithmetic reasoning, word knowledge, paragraph comprehension, math knowledge, coding speed

Noncognitive measurements: - Rotter Locus of Control Scale - Rosenberg Self-Esteem Scale

With two factors and multiple measures, the model is overidentified and can be tested.

Estimating the Factor Distribution

Key methodological point from Cunha and Heckman (2007):

  • Traditional factor analysis assumes normal factors
  • But factors may be non-normal in reality
  • Solution: Use mixture of normals \[ f(\theta_k) = \sum_{j=1}^{J_k} p_{kj} \phi(\theta_k | \mu_{kj}, \sigma_{kj}^2) \]
  • This is flexible and nests normality as a special case
  • Allows for skewness, multimodality, etc.

Separating Heterogeneity from Uncertainty

Another key use of factor models (from the Cunha and Heckman (2007) paper):

  • Observed wage variance has two sources:
    1. Heterogeneity: differences in \(\theta\) known to agent at age 17
    2. Uncertainty: shocks \(\varepsilon\) unknown at age 17
  • Factor model + choice data lets us separate these
  • How? If \(\theta \in I\) (agent’s info set), it affects choices
  • If \(\varepsilon \notin I\), it doesn’t affect choices
  • By using choice data, we learn what agents knew when

Findings on Predictability

From Cunha and Heckman (2007):

  • About 80% of lifetime earnings variance is predictable at age 17
  • Only 20% is due to genuine uncertainty (“luck”)
  • Most inequality is due to heterogeneity in skills
  • This heterogeneity is measurable using factor methods

Implication: Early interventions can have large effects because most variance is in place early.

Synthesis and Policy Implications

Bringing It Together

What have we learned?

  1. Both cognitive and noncognitive skills matter enormously
  2. Noncognitive skills are often more important than we thought
  3. Skills have a production technology with:
    • Self-productivity
    • Dynamic complementarity
    • Low substitutability early in life
  4. Measurement error is severe; factor models are essential
  5. Most earnings inequality is heterogeneity (not luck)

Policy Implications

  1. Invest early: Critical and sensitive periods suggest high returns to early childhood
  2. Holistic interventions: Target both cognitive and noncognitive skills
  3. Compensatory programs: May need to be very early due to low substitutability
  4. Measurement matters:
    • Policy evaluations need to account for measurement error
    • Standard approaches may severely understate program effects
  5. Targeting: Focus on children from disadvantaged backgrounds
    • They face both low \(\theta\) and low \(I\)
    • Dynamic complementarity means early intervention is most effective for them

The Perry Preschool Program

A famous example: J. J. Heckman et al. (2010)

  • High-quality preschool for disadvantaged 3-4 year olds
  • Large, persistent effects on:
    • Educational attainment
    • Earnings
    • Crime reduction
  • Effects work through both cognitive and noncognitive channels
  • But noncognitive effects persist while cognitive fade
  • Illustrates importance of early, comprehensive intervention

    • Evidence suggests yes for noncognitive, harder for cognitive

Key Takeaways

  1. Skills are multidimensional: cognitive and noncognitive both matter
  2. Skills are produced according to a technology with important properties
  3. Early life is a critical period due to low substitutability
  4. Measurement error is a first-order problem; factor models solve it
  5. Most inequality is heterogeneity, not luck
  6. Policy: Invest early, invest holistically, target disadvantaged
